La Vignetterie.

Anglo-Pessimism

  1. It is 2.23 and I am reading the Hansard of the third reading of the Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill. This is probably not a very good idea.

  2. There are only two methods by which governments may seek to maintain the fighting spirit of a nation in war. There is the German method, whereby all fact and opinion is disseminated from one supreme fountainhead. So far that German system has worked. I don’t think it will work forever. I don’t believe that the Germans, under their method, could have survived a series of military disasters such as the British people have survived in the past two years [applause]. …There is a great deal to be said for the British way. There is nothing whatsoever to be said for the amalgation of the [British and German ways]. That was the French way, and that’s the reason the French went down.

    [A country can be] left with one of two choices, both suicidal: either explosion or invasion.

  3. On Panorama, we hear of murders in cold blood, commissioned in our name. They are related by men of arms: by them, who know what it for a friend to step on a landmine, to be shot by a Talib in disguise as a civilian, and to be discarded by a callous political class and people on coming home. It is difficult to conceive of more credible accusations outside the courtroom.

    I am no pacifist—facile or otherwise. And I am conscious of my own cowardice and impotence. I doubt I could kill; and I doubt I could bring myself to kill. Yet I expect all the conveniences of modern life built on others’ strength of will: to sleep soundly in my bed, to take a train without fear of detonation, and to know that these tasks are carried out pursuant to rules that make the price of the former conscionable.

    Panorama’s findings evoke a most difficult possibility: that one must choose either moral or strategic suicide—moral explosion, or external invasion. Of course, that is a question for the long run. Nobody will invade tomorrow.

À propos.

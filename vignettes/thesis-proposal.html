La Vignetterie.

A rough sketch of a BPhil thesis.

First come I. My name is Jowett.
There is no knowledge, but I know it.
I am Master of this College.
What I don’t know isn’t knowledge.

Children and adults generally know at least one language. At some point, they learn their native language(s); they may do so with the help of innate abilities or knowledge. Therefore, (1) whatever langauge is, and whatever it is to know language, does not preclude our so learning; (2) a theory of language, and knowledge of language, is ceteris paribus worse as a theory if it suggests that language acquisition would be too hard; and, conversely, a theory is ceteris paribus better if it suggests that it is in fact feasible to do what we manage—acquire language.

Aside: It may be legitimate to idealise in a way that suggests we cannot learn language, if the purpose of the idealisation is to come to understand linguistic phenomena other than acquisition.

Stated imprecisely, (1) and (2) may seem almost to be nostrums. They are made much more precise on what I shall call the computationalist–Chomskyan approach to language acquisition. (1) The approach largely concerns the validity of theories of universal grammar in the vein of Chomsky (also in other works); some (e.g. Nowak, Komarova, and Niyogi) argue in favour of universal grammar, others (e.g. Clark and Lappin, “Grammar”, Nativism, “Theory”, “Acquisition”) against. (2) The argument concerned takes the following schematic form (Nowak, Komarova, and Niyogi, p. 114).

Children have to deduce the rules of their native language from sample sentences they receive from their parents and others. This information is insufficient for uniquely determining the underlying grammatical principles. Linguists call this phenomenon the ‘poverty of stimulus’ or the ‘paradox of language acquisition’. The proposed solution is universal grammar.

The arguments concerned employ a formal approach, often borrowing arguments from theoretical computer science. Gold perhaps proved the first such results, though not in a Chomskyan framework. Nevertheless, learnability results are now usually taken to inform the debate about the poverty of the stimulus and nativism. Following common practice in computer science and engineering, what counts as too hard is defined in the usual terms of computer science, and so with respect to inputs of unbounded size. For example, given a list of n elements, as a function of n, how many comparisons and swaps are needed to sort it? Similarly, given a grammar of a certain size, as a function of that size, how many linguistic stimuli are needed to learn it? What if a certain (arbitrarily small) rate of error or risk of failure to learn is permitted? And how much computation, given those linguistic stimuli, is necessary?

The application of these results to the instant case of language acquisition and further debates about the nature of language and knowledge of language requires careful handling. I propose, in this thesis, to argue that the computationalist–Chomskyan approach must be justified in one of two ways. (3) The laws of computation apply to language acquisition directly—in the same way the laws of physics apply to the trajectories of cricket balls (the nomic approach). (4) Although they do not directl apply to language acquisition, they are satisfactory as idealisations (the idealising approach), as becomes clear in the light of the philosophical literature on idealisation.

(3) On the nomic approach, the answer is perfectly banal. Language acquisition is a cognitive activity. Cognition is computation. Therefore, the laws of computation are (some of) the laws of language acquisition. The applicability of these results is as banal as that of the laws of physics to the trajectory of a cricket ball.

Aside: Cartwright would have something to say about its subsequent path once it reaches the crowd. Can physics tell us that it ends up going home with someone if it flies towards the crowd?

This is, I shall argue, not accurate in the case of most of the formal approaches attempted. This is not due to any anti-computationalist view of the mind or cognition; rather, the additional assumptions about e.g. the availability of linguistic stimulus are false.

(4) The obvious retort is that these formal approaches are satisfactory as idealisations. Considered as idealisations, formal approaches’ principal purpose is to accurately reflect the structural relations between different features of language acquisition (close to the ‘minimal idealisation’ of Weisberg): cognitive difficulty, the rate of linguistic error, the availability of corrective linguistic stimulus, and so on. I shall argue that a considerable number of existing approaches, in this respect, are flawed; learnability results are too sensitive to what are prima facie unimportant features, and so are poor idealisations. This can be attributed to the habit, borrowed somewhat uncritically from computer science, of defining difficulty with respect to inputs of unbounded size.

That argument suggests a subtler understanding of the results from theoretical computer science on which they rely. I propose therefore in the later part of my thesis to examine what a different approach to formal idealisations about language acquisition might suggest about language and knowledge of language.
